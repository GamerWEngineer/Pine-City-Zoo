GIRAFFE


Giraffes are the world's tallest land animals, known for their long necks and legs. They can reach heights of up to 18 feet (5.5 meters) and have a unique spotted coat that helps them blend into their savanna habitat. Giraffes primarily feed on leaves, fruits, and flowers from tall trees, using their long tongues to grasp food.

These gentle giants are social animals, often found in loose herds. Giraffes have a unique way of walking, moving both legs on one side of their body and then the other, which gives them a distinctive gait. Their long necks allow them to reach high branches for food, but they also use them in combat, swinging their necks to strike opponents during fights.

Giraffes are herbivores and can consume up to 75 pounds (34 kg) of leaves and twigs each day. They have a specialized digestive system that allows them to extract nutrients from tough plant material. Giraffes are also known for their unique social structure. They communicate through a variety of vocalizations, including grunts, snorts, and hisses, and they can also use body language to convey messages.

Sadly, giraffes face threats from habitat loss and poaching for their skins and bones. Conservation efforts are underway to protect these magnificent creatures and their habitats. Giraffes play a crucial role in their ecosystems by helping to maintain the balance of vegetation and providing food for other animals.

Learn more about giraffes and how you can help protect them by visiting the World Wildlife Fund.
